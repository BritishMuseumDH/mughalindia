obj10=<b>The Battle of Panipat</b><br><br>AS well as the objects of Mughal warfare such as this axe, we also have detailed accounts of specific battles. The Battle of Panipat is particularly important because Babur's victory there brought an end to the sevent-five year <font color='#990000'><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/l/lodi.html','lodi')"><u>Lodi</u></a></font>dynasty and in turn, the beginning of the Mughal empire.<br><br>Babur's troops won the battle at Panipat despite his army being heavily outnumbered. Babur had only 12,000 men whereas his <font color='#990000'><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/r/rajput.html','rajp')"><u>Rajput</u></a></font> enemies had 80,000 <font color='#990000'><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/c/cavalry.html','cava')"><u>cavalry</u></a></font> and 500 war elephants. Panipat was an important city because invaders from Afghanistan had to pass through it to get to India.<br><br>Babur's use of cannon certainly helped him win. Several cannon were lashed together with bull's hide and placed at intervals along a line of wagons. He also had <font color='#990000'><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/m/musketeer.html','musk')"><u>musketeers</u></a></font> using <font color='#990000'><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/m/matchlock.html','matc')"><u>matchlocks.</u></a></font><br><br>However, battle reports are often untrustworthy on both sides. Winners want to exaggerate their success, whilst losers want to preserve some of their pride by making out that the enemy was            bigger and better than was                                            necessarily the  case.
